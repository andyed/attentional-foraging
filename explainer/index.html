The Search Results F-Heatmap, Stroke by Stroke

2,776 eye-tracked search sessions reveal that the F-pattern's horizontal bars and vertical stem are two distinct cognitive operations — drawn 1.3 seconds apart
Andy Edmonds · April 2026 · Based on AdSERP (Latifzadeh, Gwizdka & Leiva, SIGIR 2025)

dropcap In 2006, Jakob Nielsen published three heatmaps that became the most recognized image in web design. The red-yellow-blue F-shape showed where people look on a web page: across the top, across the middle, then down the left side. Twenty years later, the F-pattern still anchors how designers think about visual attention.

But a heatmap collapses time. It shows where people looked, never when or why. It treats the F as one behavior — "scanning." It's not.

We decomposed 2,776 eye-tracked search sessions from the AdSERP dataset (Latifzadeh, Gwizdka & Leiva, SIGIR 2025) and found that the F-pattern is actually two distinct cognitive operations superimposed in a single heatmap:

Important: The horizontal bars of the F are a 1.3-second survey phase — wide saccades sampling the result set before any reading begins. Pupils constrict. It's cheap. The vertical stem of the F is the evaluate phase — narrow reading saccades moving through results serially. Pupils dilate. It's expensive.

They have different saccade amplitudes (108px vs 74px), different pupil signatures (constriction vs dilation), and different cognitive functions (gist sampling vs committed reading). They happen 1.3 seconds apart. In a heatmap, they look like one letter.

Orient

The eyes land before the mind engages. Median orientation time: 0ms. 58% of first fixations land directly on a result. The SERP layout is memorized from thousands of prior searches — the user doesn't need to "find" the results.

No learning effect across 60 trials per participant (ρ = 0.02, p = 0.30). The layout is a prior, not learned in-session.

This is not reading. This is the visual system executing a stored motor plan: saccade to the content area, calibrate to the luminance, prepare for sampling.

Survey

Important: The survey phase is the key finding. It has not been reported before in the search literature.

For approximately 1.3 seconds — exactly 5 fixations, regardless of what's on the page — the eyes execute wide saccades across the result set. Median amplitude: 108 pixels, compared to 74 pixels during subsequent reading.

This is gist sampling. The user is not reading results. They are building a rapid impression of what the result set contains: brands, price points, product types, relevance signals visible in peripheral vision.

The saccade amplitude transition is detectable within individual trials. Per-trial Spearman ρ between amplitude and fixation index: mean −0.114, p = 10⁻¹²⁸ across 2,754 trials. 69.6% of trials show a negative slope.

Three lines of evidence that the survey is a distinct cognitive phase, not just "the first few fixations":

Content-independent duration. Survey length does not correlate with SERP difficulty, result similarity, or any content measure we tested. It's a fixed sampling routine — approximately 1.3 seconds, always.

Scroll-decoupled. The survey ends at fixation ~5. The first scroll happens at fixation ~21. In 94.6% of trials, the survey is complete before any scrolling occurs. The user evaluates ~16 fixations of first-viewport content between survey and scroll.

No post-scroll reset. When the user scrolls to new content, saccade amplitude does not spike back up. The survey happens once, at the beginning. Scrolling triggers direct evaluation, not re-sampling.

What the pupils say

Note: Per-fixation pupil diameter (binocular average, blink-cleaned) across 2,720 trials reveals a three-phase pupillary trajectory.

Evaluate6–20Gradual recovery → 0%Cognitive work intensifies

The survey constricts pupils. It is cheap. The cognitive work comes later, during committed reading, where the pupil gradually recovers as working memory fills with candidates.

Survey vs evaluate pupil diameter: p = 10⁻¹¹⁷.

Not driven by visual complexity

Google SERPs include Maps boxes, Shopping carousels, Knowledge Panels. Does the survey phase disappear when these features aren't present?

No. Splitting 2,768 trials by right-panel presence: the amplitude drop survives in simple SERPs (107px vs 74px, p = 10⁻¹⁵⁰). Only 3.4% of survey fixations land on the right panel. The survey is cognitive gist sampling, not distraction by page furniture.

Evaluate

After the survey, the user transitions to serial reading. Saccades narrow. The eyes move within result snippets, not between them. Each result gets approximately 2 fixations and half a second — consistent from position 1 to position 8.

The flat ~220ms per-fixation duration is not a priming signal — it's an oculomotor constant. The valid measures of processing effort at this grain are fixation count and p(fixate), both of which are null within-position.

The evaluation is fair. Per-result reading depth does not decline with position during forward scanning. What declines is the probability of being evaluated at all — a function of scroll reach and available time, not position bias.

By position 6, the user is holding 5+ candidates in working memory. The cost of evaluating one more against everything already seen is climbing. Pupil dilation increases monotonically with position (LHIPA ρ = −0.90 with click position), confirming the working memory load interpretation.

Tip: For ranked list designers: the bottleneck by item 6 is working memory, not result quality. Surface differentiators early and make comparison easy.

Commit

The commit decision follows one of two paths, visible in the behavioral data:

Satisficers click early (positions 1–4). Fast scrolling, shallow fixation depth, low pupil dilation. They found something adequate and stopped. Classic information foraging: the first result that exceeds a relevance threshold wins.

Optimizers click late (positions 7–10). Deep scrolling, scroll regressions back up the page, highest pupil dilation. They evaluated the full set and selected from a complete comparison.

Warning: The people who click at position 10 are not giving up. Their pupils show the highest cognitive load of any click position. They read every result.

Position 10 clicks concentrate in homogeneous SERPs — where the results all look alike. When there aren't enough differentiating signals to form a strong preference, the rational response is to evaluate everything. The last result gets clicked not because the user gave up, but because discriminating between near-identical options requires seeing all of them.

The model

                         COMMIT        REGRESSION

                                        ↓

69% of trials include at least one scroll regression — a return to previously evaluated content. The regression rate correlates with decision time (r = 0.66). Scroll regressions are not noise; they are the behavioral cost of comparison under working memory load.

The existing literature has gone remarkably far with the single-pass, top-down examination assumption — cascade models, click models, and stopping rules that power every major search engine. But 69% of the trials in this dataset violate that assumption. OSEC adds what the data shows: examination is a multi-pass process with distinct cognitive phases, and the interesting decision-making happens not at the stopping point but at the re-evaluation point.

The same simplification, twice

The F-pattern and the cascade click model are the same simplification in different languages — one visual, one mathematical.

The F-pattern says: "Users scan in an F shape." The cascade model says: "Users examine results top-down with monotonically decreasing probability." Both assume one behavior from top to bottom. Neither accounts for the 1.3-second survey that happens before serial reading begins.

The cascade model's P(examine position k) is actually two probabilities mashed together: P(survey-sample position k) during fixations 1–5, then P(evaluate position k) during fixations 6+. They have different distributions, different cognitive costs, and different predictive signals.

This isn't a criticism. Nielsen's heatmaps, Craswell's cascade model, and the twenty years of work built on them were correct at the resolution available. You can't decompose temporal phases from aggregate spatial heatmaps. You can't separate survey from evaluate without per-fixation saccade kinematics and pupillometry at scale.

What changed is the data. Latifzadeh, Gwizdka and Leiva's AdSERP dataset — 2,776 trials with simultaneous 150Hz eye tracking, mouse tracking, scroll recording, and pupil diameter — gives enough temporal resolution and enough statistical power to see the phase structure that was always there. The saccade amplitude transition is detectable within individual trials (p = 10⁻¹²⁸). The pupil signature separates the phases at p = 10⁻¹¹⁷. These aren't subtle effects visible only in aggregates; they're robust per-trial phenomena that previous datasets were too small or too coarsely sampled to detect.

Science advances one dataset at a time.

We are watching our scientific resolution improve in real time. Datasets like AdSERP, combined with open analysis pipelines and AI-assisted exploration, let us revisit twenty-year-old findings and see the structure hiding inside them. The F-pattern was always two strokes. We just needed 2,776 trials and 150 frames per second to watch it get drawn.

Data and methods

Important: Open data, open analysis. The full pipeline — 13 reproducible notebooks, interactive scanpath replays with Scrutinizer foveated vision rendering, per-fixation pupil overlays, and all statistical tests — is at github.com/andyed/attentional-foraging. Interactive demo: andyed.github.io/attentional-foraging/.

All analysis is based on the AdSERP dataset (Latifzadeh, Gwizdka & Leiva, SIGIR 2025): 2,776 transactional product search queries from 47 participants. Interactive scanpath replays render each session through Scrutinizer, a neuroscience-based peripheral vision simulator, showing what the searcher could actually resolve at each fixation. Fixation positions are anchored to DOM elements for pixel-accurate overlay at any viewport width — extending the principle of DOM-based event logging from interaction events to gaze data.
