Orient–Survey–Evaluate–Commit

What happens in the 3 seconds between a search page loading and your first click
Andy Edmonds · April 2026 · Based on AdSERP (Latifzadeh, Gwizdka & Leiva, SIGIR 2025)
{dropcap} Every search begins the same way. The page loads, ten results appear, and within three seconds you've clicked one. But what happens in those three seconds is not what anyone assumed. For twenty years, search engine click models — the math behind every ranking algorithm — assumed you read results top to bottom, one at a time, until you found something good enough. Cascade models, dependent Bayesian models, user browsing models: all serial, all top-down, all single-pass. > [!QUOTE] > The query surrogates are viewed in strict order of their ranking in only about one fifth of the cases. > — Lorigo et al., 2008 We decomposed 2,776 search sessions from the AdSERP dataset — 47 people buying real products, tracked at 150Hz with a Gazepoint GP3 HD eye tracker — and found four distinct cognitive phases in those three seconds. --- ## Orient ==The eyes land before the mind engages.== Median orientation time: 0ms. 58% of first fixations land directly on a result. The SERP layout is memorized from thousands of prior searches — the user doesn't need to "find" the results. > [!MARGIN] > No learning effect across 60 trials per participant (ρ = 0.02, p = 0.30). The layout is a prior, not learned in-session. This is not reading. This is the visual system executing a stored motor plan: saccade to the content area, calibrate to the luminance, prepare for sampling. --- ## Survey > [!IMPORTANT] > The survey phase is the key finding. It has not been reported before in the search literature. For approximately 1.3 seconds — exactly 5 fixations, regardless of what's on the page — the eyes execute wide saccades across the result set. Median amplitude: 108 pixels, compared to 74 pixels during subsequent reading. This is gist sampling. The user is not reading results. They are building a rapid impression of what the result set contains: brands, price points, product types, relevance signals visible in peripheral vision. > [!ASIDE] > **The saccade amplitude transition is detectable within individual trials.** Per-trial Spearman ρ between amplitude and fixation index: mean −0.114, p = 10⁻¹²⁸ across 2,754 trials. 69.6% of trials show a negative slope. Three lines of evidence that the survey is a distinct cognitive phase, not just "the first few fixations": **Content-independent duration.** Survey length does not correlate with SERP difficulty, result similarity, or any content measure we tested. It's a fixed sampling routine — approximately 1.3 seconds, always. **Scroll-decoupled.** The survey ends at fixation ~5. The first scroll happens at fixation ~21. In 94.6% of trials, the survey is complete before any scrolling occurs. The user evaluates ~16 fixations of first-viewport content between survey and scroll. **No post-scroll reset.** When the user scrolls to new content, saccade amplitude does not spike back up. The survey happens once, at the beginning. Scrolling triggers direct evaluation, not re-sampling. ### What the pupils say > [!NOTE] > Per-fixation pupil diameter (binocular average, blink-cleaned) across 2,720 trials reveals a three-phase pupillary trajectory. | Phase | Fixations | Pupil change | What it means | |-------|-----------|-------------|---------------| | **Orienting** | 1–2 | +1.2% dilation | Arousal response to new stimulus | | **Survey** | 3–5 | −3.0% constriction | Low-load gist sampling | | **Evaluate** | 6–20 | Gradual recovery → 0% | Cognitive work intensifies | The survey *constricts* pupils (p = 10⁻¹¹⁷ vs evaluate). It is cheap. The cognitive work comes later, during committed reading, where the pupil gradually recovers as working memory fills with candidates. ### Not driven by visual complexity Google SERPs include Maps boxes, Shopping carousels, Knowledge Panels. Does the survey phase disappear when these features aren't present? No. Splitting 2,768 trials by right-panel presence: the amplitude drop survives in simple SERPs (107px vs 74px, p = 10⁻¹⁵⁰). Only 3.4% of survey fixations land on the right panel. The survey is cognitive gist sampling, not distraction by page furniture. --- ## Evaluate After the survey, the user transitions to serial reading. Saccades narrow. The eyes move within result snippets, not between them. Each result gets approximately 2 fixations and half a second — consistent from position 1 to position 8. ==The evaluation is fair.== Per-result reading depth does not decline with position during forward scanning. What declines is the *probability of being evaluated at all* — a function of scroll reach and available time, not position bias. > [!MARGIN] > The flat ~220ms per-fixation duration is an oculomotor constant, not a measure of processing effort. By position 6, the user is holding 5+ candidates in working memory. The cost of evaluating one more against everything already seen is climbing. Pupil dilation increases monotonically with position (LHIPA ρ = −0.90 with click position), confirming the working memory load interpretation. > [!TIP] > **For ranked list designers:** the bottleneck by item 6 is working memory, not result quality. Surface differentiators early and make comparison easy. --- ## Commit The commit decision follows one of two paths, visible in the behavioral data: **Satisficers** click early (positions 1–4). Fast scrolling, shallow fixation depth, low pupil dilation. They found something adequate and stopped. **Optimizers** click late (positions 7–10). Deep scrolling, scroll regressions back up the page, highest pupil dilation. They evaluated the full set and selected from a complete comparison. > [!WARNING] > The people who click at position 10 are not giving up. Their pupils show the highest cognitive load of any click position. They read every result. Position 10 clicks concentrate in homogeneous SERPs — where the results all look alike. When there aren't enough differentiating signals to form a strong preference, the rational response is to evaluate everything. The last result gets clicked not because the user gave up, but because discriminating between near-identical options requires seeing all of them. --- ## The model ``` Page load → ORIENT (0ms) → SURVEY (~1.3s, 5 fixations) ↓ EVALUATE (serial reading) ↓ ┌─────┴─────┐ COMMIT REGRESSION (click) (scroll back up) ↓ RE-EVALUATE ↓ COMMIT ``` > [!ASIDE] > 69% of trials include at least one scroll regression — a return to previously evaluated content. The regression rate correlates with decision time (r = 0.66). Scroll regressions are not noise; they are the behavioral cost of comparison under working memory load. The existing literature models SERP examination as a single forward pass with a stopping decision. OSEC says it is a multi-pass process with distinct cognitive phases, and the interesting decision-making happens not at the stopping point but at the *re-evaluation* point. --- ## Data and methods All analysis is based on the AdSERP dataset (Latifzadeh, Gwizdka & Leiva, SIGIR 2025): 2,776 transactional product search queries from 47 participants with simultaneous eye tracking (Gazepoint GP3 HD, 150Hz), mouse tracking, scroll recording, and pupil diameter. > [!IMPORTANT] > **Open data, open analysis.** The full pipeline — 13 notebooks, interactive scanpath replays with foveated vision rendering, and all statistical tests — is at github.com/andyed/attentional-foraging. The interactive demo renders each search session through Scrutinizer, a neuroscience-based peripheral vision simulator, showing what the searcher could actually resolve at each fixation.
